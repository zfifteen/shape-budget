## ELLIPSE ECCENTRICITY AS A RATIO COMPRESSION GOVERNOR: THE "SHAPE BUDGET" PRINCIPLE

When two synchronized processes each grow at the same rate but start from different positions, the shape of every possible combination between them is fully determined by how far apart the starting positions are relative to the maximum reach of either process.

Standard ellipse theory says the focal-sum constant (2a) defines the curve. What that framing misses is that the starting positions of the two centers relative to the maximum sum are not a free parameter: they set a compression ratio that controls how much of the total "reach budget" is consumed by the separation between origins, leaving the rest available to generate curvature.

When the two centers are far apart relative to the maximum radius either circle can reach, the resulting ellipse is highly compressed into a thin, elongated shape because most of the reach budget is already spent just bridging the gap between origins. The intersection region is forced into a narrow zone.

When the two centers are close together relative to the maximum radius, the reach budget is barely taxed by the separation, so the intersection traces a nearly circular ellipse. The curvature is low because there is plenty of budget left over to generate a wide, symmetric locus.

The non-obvious implication is that eccentricity is not a property of the curve itself in isolation; it is a readout of a resource-allocation process occurring between two competing expansions. The shape of the ellipse is the residue of what is left after the fixed separation cost is paid.

This predicts that in any physical system where two expanding wavefronts, influence zones, or accessibility radii originate from separated sources and interact, the aspect ratio of their combined locus will scale predictably with the ratio of inter-source distance to maximum individual reach. If you know the separation and the maximum radius, you can compute the ellipse shape before ever observing it.

A system near eccentricity zero (near-circular) is one where separation is trivially small compared to reach, meaning the two origins are functionally co-located relative to their capacity. A system near eccentricity one (near-parabolic) is one where the fixed separation cost has consumed almost all available reach budget, leaving almost no surplus to generate curvature.

This reframes standard eccentricity (e = c/a) not as a shape descriptor but as a reach-budget depletion index, and that reframing makes it useful in domains, such as epidemiological spread overlap, dual-source signal coverage, or competing influence networks, where the question is not "what shape is this?" but "how much capacity is being consumed by structural separation rather than by productive expansion?"
