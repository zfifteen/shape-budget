## ELLIPSE ECCENTRICITY AS A RATIO COMPRESSION GOVERNOR: THE "SHAPE BUDGET" PRINCIPLE

When two synchronized radial processes start from different positions and jointly spend a fixed total reach budget, the shape of every possible combination between them is fully determined by how far apart the starting positions are relative to that total budget.

Standard ellipse theory says the focal-sum constant (2a) defines the curve. What that framing misses is that the starting positions of the two centers relative to that fixed total are not a free side detail: they set a compression ratio that controls how much of the total "reach budget" is consumed by the separation between origins, leaving the rest available for transverse spread.

When the two centers are far apart relative to the total reach budget, the resulting ellipse is highly compressed into a thin, elongated shape because most of the budget is already spent just bridging the gap between origins. The intersection region is forced into a narrow zone perpendicular to the line joining the centers.

When the two centers are close together relative to the total reach budget, the separation tax is small, so the locus traces a nearly circular ellipse. Most of the budget is still available for wide, symmetric spread, and very little compression is needed.

The non-obvious implication is that eccentricity is not just a property of the curve viewed in isolation; it is a readout of a resource-allocation process occurring between two competing expansions. The shape of the ellipse is the residue of what is left after the fixed separation cost is paid.

The clean mathematical heart of the idea is simple: eccentricity e = c/a is the allocation readout, and the normalized width residue b/a = sqrt(1 - e^2) tells you how much transverse freedom remains after the separation load is accounted for.

This means that under the constant-sum two-source process, normalized shape is governed by one control ratio: separation divided by total reach budget. If you know the source separation d = 2c and the total budget S = 2a, then e = d/S tells you where the system sits on the compression spectrum before you ever draw the full curve.

A system near eccentricity zero is one where separation is trivially small relative to the total budget, meaning the two origins are functionally co-located relative to their capacity. A system near eccentricity one is one where the fixed separation cost has consumed almost all available budget, leaving only a thin transverse residue.

This reframes standard eccentricity not as a passive shape descriptor but as a reach-budget depletion index. In that form, it becomes useful anywhere a system can be approximated as two offset sources operating under a fixed total budget, because the question stops being "what shape is this?" and becomes "how much capacity is being consumed by structural separation rather than by productive spread?"
